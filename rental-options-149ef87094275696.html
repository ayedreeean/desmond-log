Option 1: Hire a Property Manager

Keep the CA properties, replace family with a professional — simplest fix for the real problem

Upfront cost $0
Tax bill $0
New annual income ~$52,000–$53,400/yr ($4,350–$4,450/mo)

Advantages

  • Solves the family dependency issue immediately
  • Keeps Prop 13 protection ($6,400/yr vs $28,600+ in TX)
  • Highest ongoing income of all three options
  • No tax hit whatsoever
  • Existing tenants stay — zero disruption
  • Stepped-up basis at inheritance (same as other options)
  • Reversible — can always sell later if it's not working

Concerns

  • Still a landlord (even if hands-off)
  • PM may flag habitability issues on older property
  • Two properties in two cities = potentially two PMs
  • Cash-paying tenants may need formalized lease

About the PM & Code Concerns

  • A PM's job is to manage what exists, not demand upgrades. They need basic habitability (working plumbing, electrical, heating) — not a remodel.
  • Look for a smaller, local PM in the San Gabriel Valley who handles older properties — not a corporate chain.
  • Be upfront when interviewing: "We have long-term tenants, minimal turnover, we want stability."
  • A good PM will see 10-year tenants with zero vacancy as a dream portfolio — they won't rock the boat.
  • Under California law (AB-291), a PM cannot ask about or act on tenants' immigration status. This actually protects everyone.
  • If the third unit at Rosemead is unpermitted, discuss this honestly with the PM upfront.

  • The 45-day rule: After selling CA, they have 45 days to identify the TX replacement property and 180 days to close. This creates time pressure.
  • Boot minimization: Finding a home closer to $1.5–1.65M would reduce or eliminate the taxable boot.

  • However — this requires stomach for market ups and downs

💡 Our Recommendation

Start with Option 1. Hire a local property manager in the San Gabriel Valley. This solves the actual problem — the family dependency — for about $550–660/month. It keeps the Prop 13 protection (saving $22,000/year vs Texas), maintains the highest income, costs nothing upfront, and is completely reversible.

The stepped-up basis at inheritance works the same regardless of which option you choose. So there's no tax advantage to doing a 1031 exchange — the capital gains are eliminated at inheritance either way.

If after hiring a PM, you still want out of CA real estate entirely, you can always revisit Options 2 or 3 later. But there's no financial urgency to make that decision now.

One thing to look into: If the third unit at Rosemead is unpermitted, it's worth understanding that situation before hiring a PM. A local real estate attorney can advise on the best way to handle it — this is independent of which option you choose.
